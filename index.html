📑 UK Landlord Compliance Requirements

  • EICR (Electrical Safety): Every 5 years. Required for all tenancies. Fines up to £30,000.
  • Gas Safety Certificate: Annual check by Gas Safe engineer. Fines up to £6,000.
  • EPC (Energy Performance): Minimum E rating required. Valid for 10 years. EPC C required by 2030 under the Decent Homes Standard 2026.
  • Smoke Alarms: Required on each floor. Must be tested at start of tenancy.
  • CO Alarms: Required in any room with solid fuel burning appliance.
  • Right to Rent: Check all tenants aged 18+ have right to rent in UK.

🏛️ Renters' Rights Act — Key Changes from 1 May 2026

🏠 Tenancy Changes

  • Section 21 abolished: No-fault evictions end. Landlords must use Section 8 grounds. Exclusions: Annual rent above £100,000, lodger agreements, company leases, and Rent Act 1977 regulated tenancies are not covered by the RRA.
  • Fixed terms end: All ASTs convert to periodic assured tenancies. Break clauses in existing agreements become unenforceable — must be exercised before 1 May 2026 (Section 1 RRA). No need to re-serve compliance documents (Gas Safety Certificate, EICR, EPC) or re-register deposits — the AST and APT are treated as one continuous tenancy.
  • "Existing tenancy" definition: An "existing tenancy" means an AST signed (binding agreement) before 1 May 2026 — regardless of when the tenancy actually starts. For example, a contract signed 1 April 2026 with a 10 May move-in date is still an "existing tenancy" with full transition protections (Section 146 RRA, The Independent Landlord, 23 Apr 2026).
  • Shared ownership subletting: Shared owners who sublet their properties are now within RRA scope — they cannot serve Section 21 notices, tenancies become periodic, and they must provide the Information Sheet to their tenants. They can still evict using Section 8 if moving back in or selling (4 months' notice, tenant cannot be asked to leave before 12 months). Shared ownership leases are no longer part of the assured regime — homes can no longer be repossessed under Ground 8 for rent arrears. Minister Pennycook has written to shared ownership providers instructing them to prepare (Mortgage Solutions, 16 April 2026).

💰 Rent Rules

  • Rent arrears threshold raised: Eviction for rent arrears only possible after 3 months of unpaid rent (was 2 months).
  • Rent increases limited: Once per year only, with 2 months' notice via Form 4A (Section 13 notice, replaces old Form 4 from 1 May 2026). Contractual rent review clauses are now prohibited — you must use the statutory process. Tenants can challenge at First-tier Tribunal. Anti-retaliation: Landlords cannot evict tenants for challenging a rent increase. 52-week cooling-off: S13 notice cannot be used if rent was increased by any means (contractual review, renewal, etc.) in the 52 weeks before the notice expires (David Smith, Spector Constant & Williams, April 2026).
  • Rental bidding banned: Adverts must state rent. Cannot accept offers above advertised price.
  • Rent in advance limited: Only one month's rent at a time after agreement signed.

👤 Tenant Rights & Information

  • Anti-discrimination: Cannot refuse tenants with children or on benefits.
  • Right to request a pet: Tenants can ask. Landlords must respond within 28 days. Pet-friendly listings have already dropped 39% since January 2026 (Inventory Base) — just 5.9% of listings now accept pets.
  • Written tenancy information: New tenancies must include prescribed terms (£7,000 fine for non-compliance).
  • Information Sheet: Must give to existing tenants by 31 May 2026. Penalty: starting point £4,000 for a first breach, maximum £7,000. If the breach continues for 28+ days after a penalty is issued, it becomes a criminal offence. Repeated breaches within 5 years: up to £40,000. Exemption: If you've served a valid S21 or S8 notice before 1 May 2026 and possession proceedings are ongoing, the tenancy stays as fixed-term and the Information Sheet is not required until/unless the notice expires (OTS Solicitors, Lendlord, Burges Salmon, April 2026).
  • Proof of delivery: A "Sent" folder is not enough to prove you delivered the Information Sheet. Keep read receipts, signed copies, or proof of postage. Email delivery alone may not satisfy the legal burden of proof (Landlord Today, March 2026).
  • 📋 New official RRA forms published (30 April 2026): Gov.uk has published watermarked previews of the new tenancy forms that replace old Section 21/Section 8 paperwork. Form 3A replaces the Section 8 notice seeking possession; Form 4A replaces Form 4 for rent increases; Form 1A/2A for succession tenancy terms; Form 5A for agricultural rent increases; Form 9A for agricultural assured tenancy opt-in. Final usable versions publish on 1 May 2026 at gov.uk/assured-tenancy-forms. You must not use the watermarked preview versions.
  • Accessible formats: Gov.uk now provides the Information Sheet in large print (20pt); Easy Read version coming soon; braille available on request via AlternativeFormats@communities.gov.uk. If you give an accessible format, you must still also give the official PDF. No translations provided. No editable/Word version available (gov.uk, April 2026).
  • Verbal agreement tenancies: If a tenancy is based entirely on a verbal agreement made before 1 May 2026 (no written record of terms), the Information Sheet cannot be used. Instead, landlords must provide written information outlining the key terms of the tenancy. This does not apply if any part of the tenancy terms were recorded in writing (mydeposits, gov.uk, April 2026).

⚖️ Enforcement & Compliance

  • Tougher enforcement: Council fines now up to £40,000 (up from £30,000). Rent Repayment Orders increased to 2 years' rent (up from 1 year). Tenants can challenge offences going back 2 years (up from 1). £60m government funding to all 317 councils for enforcement. Councils can enter premises without prior notice and access bank/accountant records (Letting Agent Today, April 2026).
  • Enforcement capacity warning: Despite the extra funding, legal experts warn enforcement may not transform overnight. Many councils already struggle with limited housing teams, and there is a national shortage of qualified environmental health officers. Compliant landlords unlikely to notice immediate changes, but rogue landlords may face increased scrutiny as the new law beds in (David Smith, Spector Constant & Williams, via Letting Agent Today, April 2026).
  • Fine collection gap: Only half of landlord fines issued by councils were actually collected in 2023/24 and 2024/25 (NRLA data). Bristol City Council — one of the most proactive enforcers — issued 30 fines totalling £226,000 but recovered just £106,000 (47%). The council's private housing team secured improved conditions in 2,717 homes in 2025, demonstrating enforcement activity but highlighting the gap between issuing and collecting penalties (BBC News, 4 May 2026).
  • Upcoming: PRS Database + Landlord Ombudsman: A national Private Rented Sector Database and mandatory Landlord Ombudsman Scheme are expected as the next phase of reform. The database is reportedly already in existence and being tested by landlords in some areas — once live, it will give local authorities definitive landlord/property records for the first time, aid tribunal rent assessments, and assist HMRC in identifying undeclared rental income. The Ombudsman is anticipated to be led by Richard Blakeway (current Housing Ombudsman), creating a single scheme covering both private and social housing. However, the Ombudsman is unlikely to launch before 2027-2028, leaving direct landlord-tenant disputes without an accessible complaints route in the interim (The Guild of Property Professionals, April 2026; Landlord Law Blog, 6 May 2026).
  • Agent preparedness: 1 in 3 letting agents have taken no preparatory steps for the RRA, with most still tracking compliance manually or not at all (Bilans Solutions/PropertyWire, April 2026). New compliance tools are emerging — Bilans Solutions launched a Reapit AgencyCloud tracker for gas safety, EICR, EPC and HMO licence monitoring.

📊 Market Data & Trends

  • Supply impact: Flatshare supply growth dropped from +13.8% to +4.2% year-on-year (SpareRoom, January 2026). UK average room rent: £747/month (barely changed). 6 cities saw 6%+ rent rises: Carlisle (+8%), Inverness (+7%), Gloucester, Durham, Worcester, Salisbury (all +6%). 1 in 4 landlords may sell up (Agent Rainmaker survey). London room rents actually fell 0.5% to £978/month.
  • London concentration: 1.1 million of the UK's 4.7 million privately renting households are in London — 28% of London households are private renters vs 17% in the rest of England. 27% of all landlords are based in London, seven times higher than the North East (4%) (English Housing Survey + English Private Landlord Survey 2024 via BBC London, April 2026).
  • Pre-ban disruption: Since Royal Assent (~6 months ago), 30% of tenants who stayed in the same rental property have had their rents increased, and 11% have been evicted or received an eviction notice (SpareRoom survey of 4,500 tenants, BBC, April 2026). Only 5 London postcodes still have sub-£800 average room rents, down from 81 in 2020 (SpareRoom). London-specific: 28% of London renters had rent increases since Royal Assent, 12% served S21 (SpareRoom/London Standard, April 2026). Student social media groups flooded with tenants seeking tips on ending tenancies at first opportunity (Metro, 1 May 2026).
  • ONS: Rent growth cooling to 3.4%: UK private rents rose 3.4% YoY to £1,377/month (12 months to March 2026) — slowest annual rise since 2022, down from 3.6% in February. Regional split: North East leads at 6.5%, London slowed to just 1.7%. England average £1,434, London £2,280, North East £772. Inflation also rose to 3.3% (from 3.0%) driven by Iran-related energy price spikes — Knight Frank warns RRA repossession risks could push rents higher despite cooling trend (ONS/Letting Agent Today, April 2026).
  • Rent growth accelerating (newly-let): Annual rental growth on newly let homes doubled from 0.5% (Feb) to 1.0% (Mar), driven by Inner London (+4.1% YoY — first reversal since 2025). London rents up 2.2% YoY; March saw a 14% increase in tenants starting their search — "one of the biggest uplifts we've ever seen" (Hamptons). 24% annual increase in tenants looking for new homes; every region recorded double-digit demand growth. 1% fewer homes available than a year ago; 33% fewer properties available vs March 2019. Renewal rents rose 3.1% YoY (up from 2.2% in Feb). Bidding above asking dropped from 56% (Q1 2021) to just 6% (Q1 2026). Hamptons warns advertised rents may shift from floor to ceiling after May 1 (Hamptons, April/May 2026).
  • Prime London rents rising (Knight Frank): Prime outer London (POL) rents increased 3% in the year to April 2026 — highest since June 2024. Prime central London (PCL) up 1.1%. Knight Frank says RRA is accelerating supply tightening as landlords "refine pricing strategies and, in some cases, exit the market." Lettings listings in both PCL and POL were 15% below the five-year average in Q1 2026. 5.9 new prospective tenants per new listing in April — highest ratio since September 2022 (Knight Frank/Letting Agent Today, 8 May 2026).
  • Whole-property rental market: Average advertised rent outside London flat (0.0%) at £1,370/pcm — first flat Q4-to-Q1 since 2017 (Rightmove, April 2026). New listings down 6% YoY. Available homes up 3% (highest since 2021). 8 enquiries per property (down from 11 last year, 29 at 2022 peak). 26% of rental listings reduced in price while advertised — highest proportion since Rightmove began tracking in 2012.
  • Buy-to-let mortgage pressure: BTL repossessions up 10% year-on-year; new BTL mortgage £1,300/year more expensive than start of March (5.45% vs 4.66%); average gross BTL yield 7.18% (up from 6.99%); 28% rise in BTL remortgages (Moneyfacts, Q4 2025 via Landlord Today, April 2026). 43% of new BTL deals now charge 5%+ rates (up from just 8% in January 2026); 78.4% of new BTL lending on interest-only basis (highest since Oct 2022); average 2-year BTL rate 5.43%, 5-year fixed 5.73% (up from 4.65% and 5.05% in Feb). Iran conflict driving oil prices and rate fears (Hamptons/Moneyfacts via LBC, April 2026). 4 in 10 landlords renegotiating interest-only deals paid off ~£30,100 towards their loan since January.
  • Market pressure: 17 households competing for every advertised rental property; 1.3mn+ households on social housing waiting lists; individual landlords account for ~81% of private tenancies in England (FTAdviser, April 2026).
  • Shelter: 2,000+ households/month protected: Analysis by Shelter estimates more than 2,000 households per month will be protected from the threat of homelessness following the abolition of Section 21. Chief executive Sarah Elliott called Day 1 "a quantum leap forward in the fight for renters' rights" after "decades of campaigning" (Shelter/Metro/Mirror, 1 May 2026).
  • Government homelessness data: 4,960 households threatened by S21 in Q4 2025: Statutory homelessness figures released 30 April show 4,960 households threatened with homelessness due to Section 21 eviction in Oct-Dec 2025 — down 12.3% from the previous quarter and 15.8% vs the same period in 2024, suggesting the pre-ban surge was already easing before abolition took effect (gov.uk/Big Issue, 30 April 2026).
  • Citizens Advice: 2,335 no-fault eviction cases in March 2026: Citizens Advice helped 2,335 people dealing with no-fault evictions in March alone — up 16% on the same time last year. The service also handled 1,800+ disrepair cases (damp/mould) and 1,000+ rent increase cases in the same month. Thousands more approached for help in the final weeks before the ban (Citizens Advice/The Guardian, 30 April 2026).
  • Pegasus Insight Q1 2026: landlord concern vs tenant reality: 80% of landlords concerned about RRA; 70% expect negative impact on their business; 77% expect broader market disruption. 4 in 5 (80%) will be more selective about tenants; 75% of those planning rent increases cite RRA as the driver. However, tenant data paints a calmer picture — typical renter has spent 5+ years in the same home, two-thirds intend to stay 4.3 more years on average, only 3% served an eviction notice in the past 12 months, and just 0.6% contested one. Pegasus Insight says the behavioural response to legislation (cautious letting, upward rent pressure) may prove more disruptive short-term than the reforms themselves (Pegasus Insight/Mortgage Strategy/Property Reporter, 1 May 2026).
  • SpareRoom: 11% of tenants evicted or served notice since Royal Assent: Survey of 4,500 tenants found 11% in England had been evicted or received eviction notice since the Act received Royal Assent on 27 October 2025 — approximately 6 months of pre-ban activity (SpareRoom/Metro/This is Money, 30 April-1 May 2026).
  • MoJ Q4 2025: possession claims fell 11% but bailiff repossessions up 3%: Landlord possession claims declined 11% year-on-year to 21,458 in Q4 2025, but county court bailiff repossessions increased 3% over the same period. Median claim-to-repossession timeline stands at 27 weeks. Accelerated claims (used for Section 21) fell 17% YoY, suggesting some landlords already adjusting behaviour ahead of the ban (MoJ/Wikipedia, April 2026).
  • Council enforcement readiness gap: FOI investigation found only 5 of 20 major councils operationally ready for 1 May 2026 commencement — Liverpool, Sheffield, Newcastle, Bristol and Brighton & Hove. Four councils admitted enforcement plans remained incomplete, while 7 failed to respond within the statutory deadline. Enforcement resourcing varies wildly: Liverpool allocated 34 full-time equivalent enforcement staff, while Bristol budgeted for just 2. Raises serious questions about whether local authorities can enforce new landlord obligations uniformly (FOI/Wikipedia, March 2026).
  • Savills: 254,000 rented homes listed for sale in 12 months: 697 former rental properties listed for sale every day to end of March 2026. BTL stock for sale up 28% vs March 2024, 9% above year-ago. London accounts for 30% of new sales instructions as former rentals vs 13% rest of GB. 14% of sold former rentals purchased by other landlords, returning to PRS. RRA has become a clear reassessment point for landlords, compounded by mortgage refixes and EPC pressures. Expect market consolidation towards smaller, more committed professional landlord pool (Savills/Letting Agent Today, 5 May 2026).
  • Landlord-agent disconnect: 76% of letting agents say admin holds back business growth; only 6% of landlords very satisfied with management services; 59% frustrated by high fees and poor value; 48% cite lack of communication as top frustration (Goodlord, April 2026).
  • Rent-in-advance ban impact: Previously, tenants could secure properties by paying rent upfront — a route used by those with poor credit, irregular income, or relying on Universal Credit. Under the RRA, landlords can only collect one month's rent in advance, meaning these applicants may struggle to prove affordability. Paragon's head of mortgages warns this will disproportionately impact more vulnerable renters, and landlords are already conducting significantly higher due diligence on prospective tenants (Louisa Sedgwick, Paragon, via Housing Unpacked/Knight Frank, April 2026).
  • Landlord sentiment (500+ surveyed): 27% plan to grow portfolios, 47% expect no changes, only 5% considering leaving PRS; 70% plan more in-depth tenant checks; 40% say S21 abolition will have greatest impact, 37% cite AST-to-periodic transition; 43% worried about being stuck with problematic tenants; 74% unsure about some or most RRA aspects; 60% need additional support; 53% plan to increase rent (37% annually); 18% looking to cut maintenance costs. Landlords want: straightforward rule summaries (62%), legal FAQs (46%), regulatory updates (44%), usable templates (41%) (Mortgage Solutions/500+ landlord survey, April 2026).
  • Agent training demand: Propertymark's RRA training course on Section 8/13 possession sold out all initial places within 30 minutes of release — additional dates now running through December 2026 (Letting Agent Today, 20 Apr 2026). Validates that agents are urgently seeking guidance on the new possession framework.
  • RRA concern levels: 82% of landlords concerned (44% very concerned); 49% planning to sell or reduce their stake in the next 12 months; 200,000+ properties could disappear from the rental market by end of 2026; 44% cite regulatory pressure as the single biggest barrier to growth (14% say it dwarfs taxation); 93,000 BTL landlords (6% of market) predicted to sell during 2025/26 (Goodlord, April 2026).
  • OnTheMarket: "landlord exodus" claims unfounded — steady 2-year rise: Converted listings (homes advertised for rent then relisted for sale within 6 months) rose 94% over two years to January 2026. March 2026 saw the sharpest monthly jump — up 40% on February and 120% higher than March 2024. However, OnTheMarket argues the trend predates RRA, driven by interest rates since 2022, tax treatment changes, rising maintenance/insurance costs, and alternative investment appeal. RRA is "a tipping point for some" rather than the sole cause — sector in transition rather than reacting to legislation in isolation (OnTheMarket/Property118, 5 May 2026).
  • Investec: London sell-off sharpest in Q1 2025, predates RRA: The sharpest rise in London rental-to-sale listings was Q1 2025 (49.9% of London sales were previously rented, up from 32.4% in Q1 2024) — predating both the RRA Bill's passage and the Act itself. Only 1 in 10 sold properties were re-let in Q2-Q3 2025, indicating permanent stock reduction. London listing numbers stayed elevated in Q1 2026 at 113,707 (up 15.5% on Q1 2024, 18% on Q1 2025) — more churn but shrinking underlying stock. Median London rents stable at ~£2,200 across all three quarters (Q1 2024–Q1 2026). Well-capitalised professional landlords acquiring units as part-timers exit (Investec/Landlord Today, 6 May 2026).
  • Allsop survey (1,000+ landlords): 70% raising rents, 42% unlikely to continue: 70.3% plan rent increases to cover compliance costs; 41.7% unlikely or very unlikely to continue letting (51.8% for single-property landlords); 48.4% planning to reduce exposure or exit entirely; only 19.3% feel ready for RRA. Professionalisation gap stark: 62.5% of single-property landlords reducing/investing vs just 36.8% of 26+ property landlords; 44% of large landlords plan to increase or maintain investment vs 25% of single-property landlords. Market consolidating towards professional operators (Allsop/Letting Agent Today, 5 May 2026).
  • Landlord selling intent vs reality: While Goodlord reports 49% of landlords intend to sell, actual market data shows the opposite trend — the share of properties listed for sale that were previously rented fell from 22.5% (Q1 2025) to 12.4% (Q1 2026), a 45% year-on-year reduction (TwentyCi, 492,000+ transactions). London saw the sharpest decline at 51% (inner London 52%, outer London 45%). Only 11% of sold former rentals in London were re-let by Q1 2026; just 6% outside London. Activity returning closer to historic norms (TwentyCi/Property118, 27 April 2026).
  • Auction House: 70% surge in tenanted property sales: Auction House saw a 70% increase in tenanted properties going under the hammer in April 2026 vs April 2025, driven by landlord exits ahead of RRA. Well-capitalised investors acquiring at 30-40% below vacant possession value — a Newcastle tenanted property attracted 86 bids, selling for £219,000 against a £115,000 guide. Buying agent Eccord warns that landlords who serve notice to sell but fail to complete cannot re-let for 12 months under RRA rules, creating risk of extended void periods with no rental income (Auction House/Landlord Today, 5 May 2026).
  • No widespread rent front-loading before RRA: Inventory Base analysis of ONS data found average rents in England rose just 0.77% in Q1 2026 (to £1,434/month), slightly below the 0.80% increase in Q1 2025. Only 26.9% of England's 294 local authority areas recorded faster rental growth vs the same period last year; 73.1% saw slower growth. London, Yorkshire & Humber, and South West were the only regions with faster growth. Suggests landlord fears of pre-RRA rent hikes were largely unfounded (Inventory Base/The Intermediary, 5 May 2026).
  • Compliance readiness gap: 84% of landlords scored at-risk or high-risk on Togal's Compliance Scorecard (average score 38%); 58% scored below 40%. Key issue: lack of evidence/documentation trails, not failing to do things — the RRA shifts burden of proof to landlords to demonstrate compliance with time-stamped records (Togal/Letting Agent Today, 23 Apr 2026).
  • Agent readiness gap: 89% of agents feel ready for RRA, but only 61% feel ready to handle eviction processes. 33% of agents think RRA will be bad for business; 18% expect to leave lettings within 5 years (Goodlord Leaders' Forum snap poll, April 2026).
  • Councils delaying evictions: Councils are advising tenants to remain in properties until physically removed by bailiffs, contravening government homelessness guidance. Landlords must obtain court order then apply for bailiff — a process taking months while arrears build. Section 8 claims are "more complex, easier to challenge, and carry greater scope for adjournments if paperwork is not completed precisely" (Paul Shamplina, Landlord Action; Nathan Emerson, Propertymark; The Negotiator, 23 Apr 2026).
  • Court delay reality: MoJ data shows median wait of 26 weeks from claim to repossession (up from ~16 weeks a decade ago). Average rent loss per property: £12,000 nationally and £19,000 in London (NRLA). BBC case study: one landlord waited 11 months for bailiff appointment after court order, with tenant £15,000 in arrears. Under RRA, S21's "paper-based" administrative route is gone — all possession claims now require court hearings if tenants challenge (BBC, 29 Apr 2026).
  • Rent guarantee insurance surge: 76% of landlords more likely to seek rent guarantee cover because of RRA; median possession time now 27 weeks; landlords face 4+ months lost income before S8 proceedings begin; claim durations forecast to rise 75-100%, legal expenses volumes up to 200%. Ceta launching 12-18 month cover (from £110) underwritten by Addept with £50k legal expenses — standard 6-month cover no longer sufficient (Ceta/Mortgage Solutions/The Intermediary, April 2026).
  • 400,000 landlords uninsured: Alan Boswell Group estimates 400,000 UK landlords operate with no insurance at all. Under RRA, an eviction that "once cost a few hundred pounds to initiate now can easily cost thousands in legal fees alone" before counting lost rent. Brokers shifting rent guarantee from optional add-on to standard offering. Industry expects tighter underwriting as loss frequency rises (Insurance Times, 6 May 2026).
  • Tenant education push: Generation Rent staging "Renters' Rights Awareness Week" from 27 April–2 May 2026 — free webinars for tenants covering council enforcement powers, eviction changes, rent rise limits, pet rules, student accommodation, and unsafe homes. Chief executive Ben Twomey: "there are so many new rights coming your way" (Landlord Today, 22 Apr 2026). Expect increased tenant awareness of RRA rights from 1 May.
  • Renter awareness gap: 78% of renters don't know how the RRA impacts their rights (up from prior Housing Hand surveys), with only 22% understanding — itself down from 25% a year earlier. 88% did not know the implementation date three months before commencement, only 3% heard about it from their landlord, and 42% find the UK rental market "confusing" (Housing Hand survey of 1,700 renters incl. students and professionals, May 2026). Of the 22% who understand the Act, 20% say they will choose to rent a different type of property and 27% may consider doing so. 37% of UK renters still unaware of the RRA, and 55% say they don't understand how the new rules will affect them; 46% did not know that fixed-term tenancies will automatically convert to rolling periodic agreements (Hiscox survey of 500 renters, April 2026). Only 18% of tenants feel fully confident in their new rights as renters, and 38% say better information about the RRA would improve their renting experience (Goodlord survey of 2,650 landlords, agents and tenants, April 2026 via Landlord Today). Tenant awareness will grow in real-time as the Act beds in.
  • Government disputes S21 surge: Housing minister Matthew Pennycook claims "no spike in Section 21 notices ahead of implementation" — MoJ data shows 17% decrease in accelerated possession claims Q4 2025 vs Q4 2024, and overall landlord possession claims fell 11% YoY to 21,458 in Q4 2025. However, bailiff repossessions rose 3% over the same period. Contradicts Acorn's data (1 in 3 cases now S21, up from 1 in 5) and SpareRoom (11% of tenants evicted or served notice since Royal Assent). Written parliamentary answer 10 April 2026 (Property118, 22 Apr 2026; MoJ/Wikipedia).
  • Competitor alert: COHO (6,000+ HMOs, 55,000+ tenants) launched free Assured Tenancy Agreement templates — HMO single-room, HMO joint, HMO single-let. Solicitor-reviewed, auto-populated from onboarding data. Phase 2 will add dynamic clause generation, room rate prompts, local market rent data, and move-in payment safeguards (Letting Agent Today, 22 Apr 2026).
  • Holiday-let pivot: Dorset holiday letting agencies reporting "real uptick" in enquiries from long-term landlords considering switching to short-term holiday lets. Property manager Blake Langhorn: "conversations have shifted noticeably" — smaller portfolio landlords doing "the maths" on whether RRA compliance burden is worth it vs more flexible holiday let model in areas with strong tourism demand (Dorset Cottage Holidays, Bournemouth Echo, 22 Apr 2026).

🎓 Student Accommodation

  • Student accommodation: Ground 4A available 1 May–30 July 2026 for student HMOs. PBSA exempt from assured tenancy regime. Two-step process required: (1) serve written notice by 31 May 2026 that you could use Ground 4A, then (2) serve possession notice between 1 May and 30 July 2026. Notice period reduced to 2 months (from 4 months) for Ground 4A notices served between 1 May and 31 July 2026 (gov.uk regulations, David Smith SC&W, April 2026).
